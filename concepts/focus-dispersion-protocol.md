# Focus-Dispersion Protocol

## Overview

A framework for maintaining professional focus by understanding and working *with* the brain's explore-exploit dopamine dynamics, rather than fighting them. Developed by [[rian-doris]] through personal experience and neuroscience research.

## Core Problem: Dispersion vs. Distraction

| | Distraction | Dispersion |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Getting pulled off-task | Getting pulled off-pursuit |
| **Cost** | Minutes/hours | Months/years |
| **Detection** | Obvious | Sneaks up — each pursuit feels productive |
| **Solution** | Block notifications, eliminate triggers | Redirect exploration drive externally |

Dispersion is triggered by **success itself**: once the brain learns to expect reward from a venture, anticipatory dopamine spikes for *new* opportunities, not current ones. This is why ambitious people scatter when things start going well.

## The Neuroscience

### Dopamine Mechanics

- Dopamine fires at **reward-prediction cues**, not rewards themselves
- Success → brain learns cue-reward association → dopamine shifts to novel cues
- Result: the better current venture performs, the louder the pull toward *something new*

**Brain structure**: Nucleus accumbens (NAc) — subcortical reward/motivation center — generates anticipatory dopamine that hijacks focus.

### Who Is Most At Risk

Entrepreneurs score high in **Openness to Experience** (Big Five personality). This trait:
- Increases sensitivity to novelty
- Strengthens anticipatory dopamine hit from new pursuits
- Makes dispersion mechanics more severe than average

### Three Compounding Biases

1. **Novelty bias** — chasing new pursuits before mastering current ones
2. **Scarcity bias** — anxiety about missing limited opportunities
3. **Present bias** — overvaluing immediate wins over long-term compounding

## The 3-Phase Protocol

### Phase 1: Pick One Pursuit

- Run a **temporal audit**: list all current and fantasy pursuits; stretch timeline to 30 years
- The pursuit that surfaces as the answer becomes the anchor
- Don't be fooled by excitement of new ventures — from outside they look like straight lines; up close they fracture into exponential subtasks

### Phase 2: Cut the Cargo

- Apply **"does it make the boat go faster?"** filter ruthlessly
- Reference: Steve Jobs cut Apple product line 70% in 1997 → $309M profit a year later
- "Deciding what not to do is as important as deciding what to do"

### Phase 3: Persist Through the Plateau

- When outcomes become predictable → dopamine drops → brain scans for novelty
- **Don't fight the dopamine system** — redirect exploration drive externally into three channels:

1. **Strategic learning** — 2-3 books/week in adjacent domains; network at target level; intellectual novelty without dispersing professional focus
2. **Environmental novelty** — relocate, travel, physical projects; uncertainty triggers dopamine outside the business
3. **High-flow pursuits with real failure risk** — BJJ, rock climbing, competitive tennis; must have meaningful failure risk to generate sufficient dopamine

**Key insight**: Flow has carryover effect. A high-flow lifestyle protects a low-flow business phase. "When work stops being exciting, make your life exciting instead."

## Key Quotes

- "Ten years of focus turns ordinary talent into something no one can compete with. Ten years of dispersion turns extraordinary talent into a long list of things that almost worked."
- "Focus compounds. Dispersion dissipates."
