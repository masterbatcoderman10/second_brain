# Mark Cuban Is Right About AI Agents. The Real Money Is 1 Vertical, 1 Workflow, 1 Painful Problem.

Mark Cuban Is Right About AI Agents. The Real Money Is 1 Vertical, 1 Workflow, 1 Painful Problem.

Mark Cuban is right about AI agents.

Most people will still miss the real opportunity.

The viral version of the AI agency playbook is easy to repeat.

Pick one vertical.
Learn the workflows.
Become the team they never hired.

That part is correct.

But it is still too vague for most people to make money with.

Because the money does not show up when you become "good at AI agents."
It shows up when you can point to one painful workflow inside one business and say:

this is the thing losing you time, margin, or revenue. I can fix it fast.

That is the part most people skip.

They try to sell intelligence.
They try to sell agents.
They try to sell AI.

The real money is still much more boring than that.

One vertical.
One workflow.
One painful problem.

## 1. The post everyone saw. The part most people missed.

One of the strongest WW winners in Airtable was the Mark Cuban playbook angle.

It worked because it gave people a business model they could understand in 5 seconds:

- pick a vertical

- learn the flows

- become the missing AI team

But the most important part of that post was not the quote.

It was the examples.

A pool company losing jobs because nobody followed up cancellations.
A PT clinic burning 3 hours every morning on insurance verification.
A cleaning company quoting in 2 days while the faster competitor quoted in 2 hours.

That is why the idea spread.

Not because people got excited about agents.
Because they could suddenly see the workflow.

And once you can see the workflow, you can see the money.

## 2. The best vertical strategy is narrower than people think.

Most people hear "pick a vertical" and still go too broad.

They choose something like:

- healthcare

- real estate

- legal

- ecommerce

That is still too wide if you want to move fast.

The better move is to go one level deeper:

- PT clinics with insurance verification delays

- cleaning companies with slow quote turnaround

- pool service companies with technician routing and reminder chaos

- HVAC businesses with missed maintenance follow-up

That is where the opportunity gets specific enough to sell.

Because now you are not offering generic automation.
You are offering one outcome tied to one painful operational loop.

That changes the sales conversation immediately.

## 3. The workflow is what clients actually buy

Another top WW winner from Airtable framed it brutally:

consultants are still selling workflows that can now be built in minutes for prices that assume weeks of work.

That post landed because it named the hidden tension clearly.

Clients think they are paying for complexity.
Often they are paying for someone to identify the right workflow, package the outcome well, and deliver it without breaking production.

That is why the workflow matters more than the tool.

Take a cleaning company.
The obvious problem sounds like "we need better operations."
The real workflow might be:

- request comes in

- request gets categorized instantly

- quote gets drafted fast

- nearest team availability gets checked

- follow-up runs automatically until reply or booking

That is what they buy.
Not n8n.
Not AI.
Not your favorite stack.

## 4. The best offers are embarrassingly simple to explain

A strong workflow offer should make sense to a tired owner in under 15 seconds.

If your explanation needs a canvas, a diagram, and 4 minutes of setup language, the offer is still too messy.

The best offers sound like this:

- when a job is cancelled, the next best customer gets contacted automatically

- when a lead asks for a quote, it gets scored, routed, and followed up fast

- when a claim or insurance check comes in, the admin work gets pre-processed before a human touches it

- when an invoice goes overdue, the right follow-up sequence starts without the owner chasing it manually

That is what makes the work sellable.

And that is also what makes it easier to productize later.

## 5. One workflow beats one hundred prompts

The reason so many people stall out is that they keep collecting prompting tricks instead of owning a workflow category.

That looks productive for a while.
It is not a business.

The money starts showing up when you know one painful loop well enough to answer these questions instantly:

- what triggers it?

- what data does it need?

- what counts as urgent?

- where does a human still need to step in?

- what breaks most often?

- what ROI shows up fastest?

That is the real moat.

Not generic AI fluency.
Workflow fluency.

## 6. Why this is getting easier now

This is the other reason the top WW posts traveled.

The build speed has compressed hard.

One of the biggest-performing angles in Airtable was not just that workflows can be built faster.
It was that the difference between what agencies charge and what the actual build takes is becoming impossible to ignore.

That does not mean the work is worthless.

It means the value is moving.

The value is moving away from:

- node dragging

- repetitive setup work

- manual debugging as a billing model

And toward:

- workflow selection

- commercial packaging

- deployment confidence

- monitoring

- ownership

- iteration speed

That is why the people who win here will not be the ones with the flashiest demo.
They will be the ones who can identify the right workflow, get it live quickly, and make it reliable enough that a client trusts it.

## 7. Where Synta actually fits

This is where most people still explain the stack badly.

Synta is not the opening hook.
The workflow is the hook.

Synta is the control layer that makes fast workflow delivery much more believable.

Because the real bottleneck is rarely the first happy-path draft.
It is the part after that:

- testing against ugly real data

- fixing the branch that breaks in production

- rerunning the flow safely

- checking what actually happened at the node level

- keeping the workflow alive once it is no longer a demo

That is why I care about the MCP angle.

I want Claude connected to the real n8n system.
I want it to inspect, edit, test, fix, and rerun the workflow instead of handing me a nice-looking sketch and disappearing.

That loop is what turns a fast build into a real delivery advantage.

## 8. The actual playbook

If I was starting from zero right now, I would do this:

Step 1. Pick one narrow vertical slice

Not "healthcare."
Not "service businesses."
Pick a very specific operator pain.

Step 2. Find one workflow tied to money or time

The best ones usually live around:

- lead response

- quote turnaround

- cancellations

- no-shows

- invoice recovery

- routing and scheduling

- customer follow-up

Step 3. Make the offer outcome-based

Do not say "AI automation setup."
Say what painful thing stops happening.

Step 4. Keep V1 tight

One workflow. One commercial win. One fast before-and-after.

Step 5. Build for ownership, not demo applause

Monitoring, fallbacks, alerts, exception handling.
This is where retainers come from.

## 9. My take

Mark Cuban is right about the direction.

But the people who make money here will not be the ones tweeting hardest about agents.
They will be the ones who can walk into one vertical, identify one painful workflow, and solve one problem fast enough that the client feels the result immediately.

That is still the real game.

One vertical.
One workflow.
One painful problem.

Everything else is distribution.

If you had to pick one vertical and one workflow this week, where would you start?
