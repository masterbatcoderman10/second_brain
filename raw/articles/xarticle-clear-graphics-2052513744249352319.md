the death of "above the fold" in startup web design

"above the fold" is basically an old newspaper term that doesn't apply to phones

let me show you what actually matters:

the concept of "above the fold" comes from print newspapers
above the top half of the front page (visible without unfolding) was where the MOST important stories always went because that's what mfs saw at the newsstand

this concept got imported into web design: put everything important in the first viewport (above the fold) because people won't scroll

the problem is that it's fucking 2026 and everybody scrolls.

especially on mobile, which is between 52-64%+ of all website traffic

why the fold doesn't matter as much anymore:

- scrolling is muscle memory

people have been scrolling on phones since 2007.

it's the most natural shit people can do on a phone

swiping and scrolling are UNCONSCIOUS behaviors

nobody "decides" to scroll, they just do it

- mobile killed the fold

on a 375px wide screen, the "fold" is about 650px from the top.

that's enough for a headline, maybe a subheadline, and a button so trying to cram your entire value proposition into 650px of vertical space creates cramped asf, overwhelming heroes

the best converting pages encourage scrolling

the hero should make you curious enough to scroll

if the hero tells you everything, there's literally zero reason to keep reading

- long form landing pages outperform short ones

multiple studies show that pages with 5-7 sections convert higher than pages with 1-2 sections for high consideration purchases.

this is because more scroll depth = more touchpoints = more trust built (kinda obvious but whatever()

what to do instead:

- optimize the first 3 seconds and NOT the first viewport

the question should be "does the first thing the visitor sees make them want to keep reading?"

headline: communicates what you do and who it's for (under 10 words)

one visual element: product screenshot, hero image, or animation that reinforces the headline

one cta: visible but not the only thing on the screen

the visitor reads the headline, sees the visual, and either bounces or scrolls (hopefully scrolls lol)

the cta is there for the 5-10% who are already convinced and the other 90% need more information before they're ready to click.

- use scroll depth as a conversion metric

instead of obsessing over what's "above the fold," track how far visitors scroll.

if 80% of visitors scroll past section 3 but your cta is only in the hero and the footer, you're missing a massive conversion opportunity in sections 2-3 where engagement is highest

- design for the scroll

create visual rhythm that rewards scrolling and alternate between text heavy sections and visual sections.

also use scroll triggered animations that reveal content as the visitor moves down to make the journey feel intentional rather than cramped
