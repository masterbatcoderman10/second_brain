## Why 15 seconds is the sweet spot

A 15-second ad has one job: convert attention into action.

It doesn't need a perfect film pipeline.

It needs a tight sequence of moments.

The reason Seedance 2.0 works here is simple:

It can produce "good enough to ship" commercials fast so you can iterate like a performance marketer, not a filmmaker.

## The 4-Beat Ad Structure (copy/paste)

Every winning short commercial is basically four beats:

1) Hook (0–2s)

The pattern break.

Something that forces a pause.

Examples:

- unexpected visual
- bold claim
- "before" moment
- curiosity shot

2) Problem (2–5s)

Make the pain obvious in one moment.

Examples:

- messy hair / dull skin
- slow workflow
- annoying friction
- bad result

3) Proof (5–12s)

The most important part.

Show the product working.

Don't explain it.

Examples:

- transformation
- demo step
- output reveal
- clear "after"

4) CTA (12–15s)

One action. One line.

Keep it clean.

Examples:

- "Try it today"
- "Get started"
- "Watch the full demo"

If your ad doesn't have a proof moment, it's content not a commercial.

## The 3 commercial formats Seedance is best at

A) "Before → After" ads

Fastest to make, easiest to understand.

Best for: beauty, fitness, editing tools, productivity, AI tools, anything with a visible improvement.

B) "Demo in 3 steps" ads

Great for products people don't understand instantly.

Best for: apps, platforms, SaaS, AI tools, workflows.

C) "Reaction ads"

You show the result first, then the reaction.

Best for: anything visual (video, design, AI generations, transformations).

The "Creator ROI" mindset (what most people miss)

Creators burn time trying to make one perfect ad.

Winners make five good ads, then keep the one that performs.

That's the entire advantage of Seedance 2.0:

- generate variations
- pick the winner
- scale the concept
- repeat weekly

This is not "art."

This is creative iteration.

## The reference pack that makes ads look expensive

If you want your commercials to stop looking like AI demos, your inputs matter.

Your "reference pack" should be simple:

- 1 identity anchor (same character/product look every time)
- 1 style anchor (the lighting + color vibe)
- 1 composition anchor (how the frame is arranged)
- optional motion anchor (if you're doing fast movement)

That's it.

More references ≠ better results.

Cleaner references = better results.

## A quality checklist for 15-second ads

Before you post, check this:

- Is the hook visible in the first 2 seconds?
- Is the problem obvious without reading text?
- Does the proof moment look clean and believable?
- Is the CTA one simple action?
- Does it feel like an ad, not a "cool clip"?

If you can't answer "yes" quickly, iterate.
