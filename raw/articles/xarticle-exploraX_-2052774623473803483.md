  five steps. you can run the whole thing today.

  ---

  ## 01 — what makes a business a prospect

  here's the mental model first.

  every local business is a bucket. they pour money in ads, signs, referrals trying to fill it with customers. but most of these buckets have holes:

  leads come in and fall right through. 
  the phone rings, nobody picks up. 
  a customer finds them online, sees zero reviews, clicks the competitor. somebody visits their website and there's nowhere to book.

  your job isn't to pour more water in. your job is to find the holes and plug them.

  google maps is full of these businesses. the trick is knowing which holes to look for, so you can teach claude code to find them for you in step 03.

  here are the four signals. learn them once, and you can spot a prospect in seconds or hand the criteria to claude code and have it scan thousands:

  1. low review count: the businesses ranking at the top of any local search have 200+ reviews. the ones buried below often have under 30. those are your prospects. when a customer compares an HVAC company with 250 five-star reviews against one with 8, they pick the 250 every time, even if the smaller business does better work. low reviews = invisible. and the owner already feels it.

  2. no website linked: click into a listing. no website = a massive hole. customers who want to research before calling have nowhere to land, so they bounce to the next listing. you can fix this with a one-page site in an afternoon.

  3. no responses to reviews. if customers left nice comments and the owner never replied, that's free marketing they're ignoring. worse. it signals "nobody's home" to every future customer reading those reviews.

  4. missing basics. no photos, no hours, no description. every empty field is a reason to scroll past. listings with photos get 42% more requests for directions and 35% more clicks to their site, per google's own data.

  two or more of these signals = prospect.

  the businesses that hit this profile aren't skeptical when you reach out. they already know something is broken. they just don't know who to call. that's the whole game.

  next step: scrape a list of them.

  ---

  ## 02 — scrape the list with instant data scraper

  manually copying business names, phone numbers, and websites off google maps is the kind of work that sounds easy until you've done it 50 times.

  it's slow, it's mind-numbing, and you'll quit before you have enough leads to matter.

  the fix is a free chrome extension called instant data scraper. it reads whatever's on the screen — names, ratings, review counts, phone numbers, websites — and dumps it into a clean csv. no signup, no api keys, no code.

  here's the full setup:

  — install it. go to the chrome web store, search "instant data scraper," and hit add to chrome. takes 10 seconds. it lives in your extensions toolbar.

  — run your search. open google maps, type a category + city (we're still using HVAC in phoenix), and click "more businesses" so the full list loads on the left side.

  — scroll the list to the bottom. google only loads listings as you scroll. scroll until it stops loading, that's your full result set, usually around 100–120 businesses.

  — click the extension icon. instant data scraper auto-detects the table of listings and highlights it. if it grabs the wrong section, click "try another table" until it locks onto the business list.

  — hit "csv". the file downloads. open it in google sheets or excel.

  what you get: a spreadsheet with one row per business — name, address, phone number, website (if they have one), star rating, review count.

  the exact data you need to qualify prospects against the four signals from step 01.

  at this point you've done in 90 seconds what would've taken you three hours manually.

  but you're not done — a raw list isn't a prospect list. you still need to filter out the businesses that don't fit the criteria, and write personalized outreach for the ones that do.

  that's where claude code comes in.

  ## 03 — feed the csv to claude code (filter + write emails)

  this is where the article earns its title.

  instead of opening 100 spreadsheet rows one by one, judging each business against the four signals, then writing 100 personalized emails — you hand the whole csv to claude code and let it do all of it. in one session.

  two stages.

  stage 1: filter the prospects.

  open claude code in the folder where you saved the csv. drop in this prompt:

  ```
  Read the file `hvac-phoenix.csv`. Each row is a local business
  from Google Maps.

  For each row, score it from 0 to 4 based on how many of these
  signals it has:

   1. Review count is under 30
   2. No website listed
   3. Star rating is below 4.0 (suggests poor review management)
   4. Missing key fields (no phone, no address, etc.)

  Then visit the website (if one exists) and check whether they
  respond to reviews. If they don't, add 1 to the score.

  Output a new file called `prospects.csv` containing only the
  rows with a score of 2 or higher. Add two new columns:
  `signal_score` and `signal_notes` (a short list of which
  specific holes you found, e.g. "no website, only 12 reviews,
  never replies to reviews").

  Sort by signal_score descending — best prospects on top.
  ```

  claude code reads the csv, scores every row, fetches websites where present, and writes the filtered file. on a list of 100, you usually end up with 30–50 qualified prospects, each tagged with the exact reasons they qualify. that tagging is what makes everything else work.

  stage 2 — make the calls.

  here's the thing nobody tells you: cold emails get a 1–2% reply rate. cold calls to qualified prospects book meetings at closer to 10%. it's not even close.

  the email comes later as a fallback. the real money is in the call — and you don't want claude code writing your call for you. a written script kills authenticity in the first three seconds. business owners can hear "scripted" the way a dog hears a treat bag.

  what you want instead is to know your opening 10 seconds by heart, and then trust yourself for the rest.

  open prospects.csv, sort by signal_score, and start dialing the top 20. before each call, glance at the signal_notes column for that row. that's your opener. one specific observation about their google listing. nothing rehearsed.

  the goal in those first 10 seconds isn't to pitch. it isn't to book a meeting. it's to give the owner one reason to stay on the call instead of hanging up.

  and the only reason that works is genuine specificity, you're not "an agency" calling about "their digital presence," you're a human who actually looked at their listing and noticed something real.

  three things to do in those 10 seconds:

  — say their name. "hey, is this john?" if they say yes, you've built a tiny thread of conformity that makes hanging up feel awkward.

  — lead with the observation, not the offer. "i was looking at your google listing this morning and noticed you don't have a website linked." no agenda, just a fact about them. it gets them curious instead of defensive.

  — end the opener with a question, not a pitch. "is that something you've been meaning to fix, or is it just not a priority right now?" you've handed them control. they'll tell you exactly what they care about, in their own words. now the rest of the call writes itself, because they're driving it.

  if they engage past 10 seconds, you've earned the meeting. if they don't, you move on — no harm done, the email goes out as the fallback.

  stage 3 — write the email fallback.

  for the prospects you couldn't reach by phone — voicemail twice, or a number that just rings out — you fall back to email. same observation, different channel.

  still in the same claude code session, drop in:

  ```
  Now read `prospects.csv`. For each row, write a short cold
  email tailored to that specific business based on its
  `signal_notes`.

  Rules:
  – Lowercase, friendly, no corporate filler
  – Lead with one specific observation about THEIR listing
  – Mention one thing they're doing well (find it in the data
    or infer from review snippets)
  – Offer to share a free breakdown — never ask for a call
  – Maximum 4 short paragraphs
  – Sign off as: "best, m0h"

  Subject line should reference the specific gap (e.g.
  "noticed something on your google listing" or "couldn't
  find your website").

  Output everything into a new file `outreach.csv` with
  columns: business_name, phone, email, signal_notes,
  email_subject, email_body.
  ```

  what comes back is a csv where every row has a ready-to-send email referencing that exact business's gaps. no copy-paste templating. no "hi {first_name}" energy. each one reads like you spent 10 minutes on it.

  on the HVAC-in-phoenix list, the whole flow, filter, call the top 20, email the rest — runs in about 2 hours. doing this manually would take your entire week, and most of those emails would end up sounding the same anyway because by call 40 you've run out of original observations.

  ## 04 — handle the three objections, book the meeting

  if you make 20 calls, you'll hear three objections more than any others. handle them well and you'll book 2–3 meetings per session.

  handle them badly and you'll spend a week wondering why this method "doesn't work."here they are.

  "i'm not interested." 
  this one is reflexive. it's not a real objection — most owners say it before they've processed what you said. they're saying it to whoever cold-called them yesterday, last week, this morning.

  it's muscle memory.the move: agree with them, then bring it back to the observation."yeah, totally fair. i'm not selling anything — i actually just wanted to flag the [missing website / 8 reviews / no review responses] thing because the businesses around you are getting most of the calls in the area because of it. takes 30 seconds, then you can decide if it's worth your time."

  two things are happening. you're agreeing, which removes the energy they were preparing to push back with. and you're reframing — the call is now about their problem, not your offer. half the time, that buys you another 60 seconds. that's all you need.

  "just send me an email."
  this is the most common objection on cold calls, and it's a trap. "send me an email" almost always means "i want you to leave me alone, and i know you'll never follow up." emails sent after this objection get opened maybe 5% of the time.

  the move: agree to send the email — but get a small commitment first."happy to send it over. quick question before i do — is the [missing website / low reviews / etc.] something you'd actually want to fix this month, or are you good with how things are running? just so i know what to put in the email."

  if they say "yeah, i've been meaning to look at that," you're not sending an email anymore, you're booking the next conversation. if they say "nah, i'm good," at least you know not to waste your time emailing.

  "how much?"
  if they ask this, you've already won, they're qualifying you. but this is also where most people lose the call. they panic, blurt out a number, and the prospect goes quiet because the number is either too high (sticker shock) or too low (sounds amateur).

  the move: don't answer yet. you don't know enough to price it."good question, depends on the setup. some of this is a one-time fix, some of it's ongoing. honestly the easiest way is i hop on a quick 15-minute call this week, look at your setup with you, and send you a number that actually fits. does tomorrow at 10 work, or is later in the week better?"

  you've done three things. you've signaled this isn't a templated pitch. you've made the price feel custom (which it should be). and you've slid into the booking ask.

  booking the meeting.
  when a prospect engages past 60 seconds, they're asking questions, they're agreeing the gap is real, they're not trying to get off the call, book the meeting. don't drag it out. don't try to close on the first call.

  the ask is three sentences:
  "i'd love to put together a quick walkthrough for your specific setup, what's working, what the gaps are, what fixing them would actually look like. it's free, takes 15 minutes, and you can decide at the end if you want me to handle it. does tomorrow at 10am work, or would later in the week be better?"

  three things to notice:

  - the offer is a "walkthrough," not a "meeting" or a "call." "meeting" sounds corporate. "walkthrough" sounds like a favor. business owners agree to walkthroughs.

  - you give them an out at the end. "you can decide at the end if you want me to handle it." this kills the worry that they're agreeing to be sold to. you've made the meeting feel low-stakes because for them, it is.

  - you offer a specific time, not "what works for you?" "tomorrow at 10" forces a yes/no. "what works for you?" forces them to do mental work, and people in the middle of their workday don't want to do mental work for a stranger. if 10 doesn't work, they'll counter with a time — and that's still a yes.once they say yes:

  - confirm the time and date out loud. "great — tomorrow, wednesday the 14th, at 10am your time."

  - get their email right then. "what's the best email to send the calendar invite to?" they're already saying yes — getting the email is now trivial.

  - send the calendar invite within 5 minutes of hanging up. people who agreed warmly at 9:47am can cool off by 10:30am. the calendar invite locks it in.that's it. you've turned a cold number on a spreadsheet into a booked meeting with a qualified prospect. tomorrow at 10, you show up and close. which is what step 05 is about.

  ## 05 — close the meeting, deliver the work

  tomorrow at 10am the prospect shows up. the meeting has three jobs in three moves.

  show them the gap. screen-share their google listing. show the empty website field, the 12 reviews, the unanswered review thread. don't tell them they have a problem — they already admitted it on the call. just make it visible.

  show them the fix. pick one of three offers based on the gap they admitted to:

  — a one-page website for businesses with no website. phone number, services, hours, quote form. an afternoon's work with a no-code tool.

  — review automation for under-30-review businesses. a system that texts every customer a review link after the job is done. doubles their review count in 60 days.

  — an ai receptionist or missed-call text-back for businesses where the phone goes unanswered. ~62% of calls to local service businesses don't get picked up — and those callers don't call back. catch the lead before it's gone.

  one gap, one fix. don't pitch all three.

  ask for the sale.

  "so this is what i'd build for you. want me to put it together this week?"

  stop talking. let the silence sit. it's uncomfortable on purpose.

  if yes → invoice within the hour, start the same day, send a progress update within 48 hours. small businesses get cold feet — fast delivery beats the doubt before it forms.

  if "let me think about it" → "what would you need to know to decide today?"

  if no → keep it warm, email them again in six months.

  finish the job. ask for a testimonial. ask for two referrals.

  that's the whole method. google maps to find the businesses. instant data scraper to build the list. claude code to filter and write the email fallback. the call to book the meeting. the meeting to close.

  none of it requires a course or permission. step 01 is open in another tab.
