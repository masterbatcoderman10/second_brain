## The Columns That Actually Matter (My Custom "Million a Month" Preset)

Before getting into how to set up a new campaign, you need to understand which metrics to actually track because the default columns Meta gives you are mostly useless for agency advertisers running performance campaigns.

Inside Ads Manager, you can save custom column presets. The one I run is called "Million a Month" and it shows me exactly the metrics I need to make decisions on. Here's what's in it and why each one matters.

Amount Spent. Obvious but important. How much each campaign, ad set, and ad has spent. This is the denominator for almost every other meaningful calculation.

Results. This shows you how many of the conversion event you're optimising towards have been generated. For us, every campaign optimises towards website schedules (booked meetings on our calendar) because that's how we actually sign clients. We get prospects on a call, sell them a high-ticket media buying service, and that's how we make money. So our results column shows website schedules. If your agency optimises toward something else (form fills, low-ticket purchases, etc.), the results column will reflect that.

Impressions. How many times your ad has actually been shown. Different from views, which I find a bit ambiguous in how Meta calculates them (my account shows 1.6 million views and only 1.5 million impressions, which doesn't make immediate sense but isn't a metric I use for decisions anyway).

CPM (Cost per 1,000 Impressions). This tells you how expensive your traffic is. CPMs vary massively by geography. A US CPM might be 10x what an African CPM is because there's significantly more competition for those eyeballs. CPMs also vary by audience sophistication. The smaller and richer your target market, the higher your CPMs because more advertisers are bidding for the same scarce attention.

The interesting thing about CPMs is that you can actually decrease them by making better ads. Meta gives every advertiser a quality score that's a function of expected CTR, landing page experience, and how often people are reporting your ad. If your quality score is high, Meta rewards you with lower CPMs because the platform wants to incentivise good ads and push out bad ones. If your ads are annoying users, Meta throttles your CPMs up to push you off the platform. So part of optimising CPM is just making ads that don't suck.

Unique Outbound CTR. This is one of the most important metrics in my account, and it's the one most agency owners aren't tracking properly. The formula is unique clicks divided by impressions, which tells you how compelling your ad is to the people who see it. The more people who click on your ad as a percentage of impressions, the more compelling it is.

We have specific benchmarks we want to hit on CTR. For video ads, anything below 1% is essentially cooked. A 1.5 to 2% unique outbound CTR on video is good. 2%+ on video is great. Static ads tend to get higher CTRs naturally (3 to 4%+ is achievable) because they're consumed faster and the click decision happens quicker.

If your CTR is consistently below benchmark, you're either showing the ad to the wrong type of person or the ad itself isn't compelling enough. Both of these issues compound. Lower CTR leads to a worse quality score, which leads to higher CPMs, which leads to a higher CPC. Everything gets more expensive when your ads aren't compelling.

CPC (Cost per Click). Largely a function of CPMs and CTR. If your CPMs are high and your CTR is low, your CPC will be expensive. If you can move CTR up while keeping CPM stable, your CPC drops automatically. It's a downstream metric, but useful to track.

Cost per Result. This is obvious but critical. How much spend you need to generate one of the events you're optimising for. For us, this is cost per booked call. This is the metric that tells you whether the campaign is actually worth running, because at the end of the day, you're not in the business of getting clicks. You're in the business of getting clients.

Frequency. This tells you how many times on average a single person is seeing your ad. You want frequency to stay under 1.5 in a healthy cold traffic campaign. Once frequency creeps above 3, you're showing the same ad to the same people repeatedly, which is essentially a retargeting situation.

High frequency on a cold traffic campaign signals that you're tapping out your TAM. Meta is recycling your ad to people who've already seen it because it doesn't know who else to show it to. This is one of the strongest signals that you need to launch new creatives. Ad fatigue is real and frequency is the leading indicator.

Hook Rate (3-Second Video Plays per Impressions). This is what we call hook rate in the industry. It tells you what percentage of people who saw your video ad watched past the 3-second mark. You want this above 20%. Below that and your hook isn't doing its job. The viewer scrolled past before the ad had a chance to communicate anything meaningful.

I have plenty of ads in my account with hook rates under 20%. That's fine. You have to fail a lot in the lab to find the winners. Most ads will have weak hooks. The point is to keep testing until you find the ones that don't.

Unique Outbound Clicks. Useful as a raw volume metric to pair with CTR.

The metrics I prioritise most when judging an ad: unique outbound CTR, CPM, hook rate, and cost per result. And above all of them, the metric that ultimately decides whether an ad lives or dies is ROAS. If an ad has a terrible CTR, a terrible CPM, and a terrible hook rate, but it's producing a positive ROAS, you do not turn it off. The point of all these metrics is to predict and improve ROAS, but ROAS itself is the final judge.

## The Best Performing Ad in My Account

Quick context on what a winning ad actually looks like at scale.

The top-performing ad in my Paid House account spent £42,000 over its lifetime. Most of my ads spend around £2,000 to £3,000 before they fatigue and need replacing (which happens fast in the e-commerce niche because the TAM is smaller than people think and I lean heavily on bottom-of-funnel ads, something I'm trying to move away from in 2026 by adding more top-of-funnel educational content that lasts longer).

The reason this specific ad was able to scale to £42k in spend was the 2% CTR. That's elite for a video ad. CPM was decent but not exceptional. Frequency ended up high because we ran the ad for 4 to 5 months and showed it to nearly everyone in the addressable market, but the CTR was what carried it through.

For context, that ad got over 350,000 impressions in its lifetime and produced a meaningful chunk of the 737 booked meetings sitting on this account.

The takeaway: when you find a winner, milk it for everything it's worth. Most ads will be average. The one or 2 that scale are what make the entire account profitable.

## How I Set Up a New Campaign From Scratch

Now into the actual walkthrough. This is the exact process I follow every time I launch a new campaign for an agency.

Campaign Level

Click the green "Create" button in the top left. Select Leads as the objective.

You'll land on the campaign view. There are 3 levels: campaign view (what you're on now), ad set view, and ad view. Each level has its own settings and naming conventions.

The first thing to do at the campaign level is name it properly. This sounds menial but it's genuinely important. Once you start launching multiple campaigns, things get lost fast if your naming is inconsistent.

My naming convention at the campaign level:

- ABO or CBO (whichever budget structure you're using)

- The offer name

- The launch date

So a campaign might be named: "ABO – Cold Email Retainer (B2B) – Feb 6"

That's it at the campaign level. Don't overcomplicate it.

The CBO vs ABO decision. I run ABO almost exclusively. ABO means Ad Set Budget Optimisation, where you control the budget at the ad set level. CBO is Campaign Budget Optimisation, where Meta decides how to allocate budget across ad sets within the campaign.

The reason I prefer ABO is control over creative testing. With CBO, Meta can decide to put almost all the budget on 1 ad set and ignore the others, which makes it impossible to test different creative concepts cleanly. With ABO, you force Meta to give every ad set a fair shot at delivery. For agencies in the testing phase of building out their account, this matters.

Ad Set Level

This is where most of the meaningful settings live, and where Meta will try to push you into options that hurt your performance. Override the defaults.

Conversion Location. Meta now likes to hide this and push you toward multi-conversion path setups. I don't believe these get you better results. Select "Website" as the single conversion location. Single, simple, controllable.

Pixel and Conversion Event. Select your pixel. Select your schedule event (or whatever your booked-call equivalent is). Don't touch the attribution model. Don't touch any of the technical settings beneath this. The defaults are fine.

Dynamic Creative. This is a setting where Meta lets you upload multiple creatives, headlines, primary text variants, and descriptions, and the algorithm mixes and matches them automatically. In theory, dynamic creative should test more thoroughly than single ads.

In practice, I've found single ads work better. I used to run dynamic creative testing extensively and have moved away from it, especially after Andromeda. Single ads give you cleaner attribution, cleaner data, and (in my experience) better delivery. Turn dynamic creative off.

Daily Budget. My standard recommendation is $100 a day minimum per ad set. I've found $200 a day actually performs slightly better because you get results faster, but $100 is the floor.

For this walkthrough, I'd put the budget at £160 a day (approximately equivalent to $200 USD).

Start Date. Some advertisers like to set the start date a few hours in the future as best practice (in case the ad gets flagged on review, you don't want it failing to launch). Technically the right move. In practice, I usually just leave it as is and launch immediately.

Audience Settings. This is where you'll spend the most time getting the configuration right.

Country. Select your target country. For most B2B agency offers, the US is the strongest market. The UK is genuinely a tricky market for B2B ads (smaller TAM, more sceptical audience, tougher to scale), so unless you specifically need UK leads, default to the US.

Saved Audiences. Don't use any. Leave them blank.

Audience Definition. You should see this say "Broad" in the right-hand panel. This is what you want. Broad targeting means Meta's algorithm uses everything it knows about its users to find people who match your ICP, rather than you manually specifying interests or behaviours.

The reason broad targeting works is that Meta's algorithm is significantly better at finding your audience than you are. They have billions of dollars invested in their targeting infrastructure and exponentially more behavioural data than you have. Trust the algorithm, especially in 2026.

Now, with broad targeting set, be specific in 2 areas.

Age Range. Don't accept Meta's "user suggestion" defaults. Look at your last 20 customers and figure out the actual age range of people buying from you. For most B2B agency offers, the right window is 25 to 55. People under 25 typically don't have buying authority for high-ticket B2B services. People over 55 are often less receptive to the pitch-and-call sales process most agencies run.

Override Meta's age suggestions and set this manually.

Gender. Same exercise. Look at your last 20 customers. If 80% of your buyers are men, restricting to male is a meaningful efficiency improvement because you're not wasting spend on the 30% of impressions that would have gone to women. If your buyers are split, leave it as all genders.

Important note: doing both of these things still keeps your audience definition as "broad" because you're not adding interest, behaviour, or detailed demographic targeting. You're just sensibly trimming the edges of an otherwise broad audience. This is broad targeting done right, not narrow targeting in disguise.

Detailed Targeting. Leave this completely empty. Don't add any interests like "Business Administration" or "Shopify Store Owners." This narrows your audience and constrains the algorithm. Broad wins.

Placements. This is where Meta will silently sabotage your campaign if you don't override the defaults. By default, Meta has every placement turned on.

Override this. Restrict to Facebook and Instagram only. Specifically:

- Facebook Feed

- Facebook Reels

- Instagram Feed

- Instagram Reels

- Instagram Stories

- Facebook Stories

Turn off Audience Network. This is critical. The Audience Network shows your ads on third-party websites and apps, which produces complete garbage traffic. It will give you a high CTR, a low CPM, and even potentially a lot of booked calls, but the lead quality is absolutely terrible. It will skew your campaign metrics, ruin your learnings, and waste your ad spend on prospects who will never convert.

The Audience Network exists to help Meta extract more spend from advertisers, not to help you get quality leads. Turn it off every time. There's also a small toggle that says something like "include placements not specified" or similar, make sure that's also turned off so Meta can't silently add the Audience Network back in.

Ad Set Naming. Same convention as the campaign level. Mine looks like:

"Schedule – Batch 1 – US – 25-55"

You're naming what the ad set is optimising for, which batch of creatives is in it, the country, and the age range. If you have 5 batches running across 2 countries with different age ranges, the naming makes it instantly clear which is which.

Ad Level

Final level. This is where the actual creatives live.

Naming Convention. I use a numbered system tied to the batch. If this is the first batch and the first ad in it, the ad is named "101." If you launch 20 ads in batch 1, they'd be 101 through 120. If you launch a second batch, the ads would be 201 through 220. Clean, sequential, easy to track.

Why 20 ads per batch? Post-Andromeda, Meta rewards creative variety inside an ad set. The standard recommendation now is to launch with 20 unique creatives per ad set so the algorithm has enough variety to match different audience segments with different ads. This is more demanding than it used to be (you used to be able to launch with 5 to 10 ads and be fine), but it's where the platform is in 2026.

If 20 unique creatives feels unrealistic for your current production capacity, launch with what you can sustain. 8 to 12 unique creatives is a workable starting point. The compounding effect of consistent creative production over 6 to 12 months matters more than hitting a perfect creative volume in any single batch.

Connect Your Facebook Page and Instagram Account. Make sure both are linked to your Business Manager. If you don't have a business Facebook page, create one (you can't run ads from a personal profile). If you don't have an Instagram account, connect one (your ads look more credible when they're being served from an Instagram account that has actual content on it).

Ad Setup.

Meta will ask if you want to create an ad with an instant form. Select "None." No instant forms. We've covered this in other content but the short version is that instant forms produce dramatically lower lead quality than landing page traffic, even though they look like they're converting at higher volumes.

Destination URL. Your funnel URL, not your website. You're sending traffic to the specific landing page built for this offer, not the homepage of your agency.

Site Links. Turn these off. Meta tries to add additional secondary links to your ad. Don't let them. You want one funnel link, one funnel destination, no distractions.

Creative Upload. Add your image or video creative. For this example, let's say it's a video ad in 4:5 format (which is what I tend to use for talking head ads because the extra vertical real estate gives you space for a banner at the top, which historically has improved CTR for us).

Set the placement size to "Original." This serves the ad at the dimensions you uploaded across both Facebook and Instagram.

Primary Text, Headline, and Description. This is the copy that appears with the ad. The primary text is what people see first (the text that appears above or beside the video on the feed), so this is where the most important copy goes.

Up to 5 variations of each. Meta lets you upload up to 5 versions of primary text, 5 headlines, and 5 descriptions. There's no harm in adding all 5 of each because it gives Meta more variety to test.

Important note: the first thing people see is the primary text, not the headline. The headline appears at the bottom of the ad next to the CTA button. If you're spending hours obsessing over headline copy and only minutes on primary text, you have it backwards.

Call to Action. Set this to "Learn More." I've tested almost every CTA option Meta offers and Learn More consistently outperforms the alternatives for B2B agency offers. "Sign Up," "Book Now," and the more aggressive CTAs reduce CTR because they create more friction at the click decision. Learn More feels lower commitment and gets clicked more.

AI Improvements. Turn all of these off. Meta tries to apply AI enhancements to your creative (auto-cropping, music suggestions, layout adjustments, etc) and they're consistently bad. Leave your creative exactly as you uploaded it.

Hyros Parameters. If you're using Hyros (hyros(.com)) for attribution (which you should be, especially if you're spending more than £3,000 to £5,000 a month), add the Hyros parameters to your destination URL so the platform can track conversions properly. This is the single best investment you can make for improving your attribution data and therefore your decision-making.

Click Done. Then if you have multiple creatives to upload, use the "Quickly Duplicate" feature inside the ad set to duplicate the ad and replace the creative with a different one for each variation. Saves time vs setting each ad up from scratch.

## What to Do After Launch

Once the campaign is live, do not touch it for 5 to 7 days. Meta needs time to learn from the data. Every time you make a change, you partially reset the algorithm's optimisation process.

What you're looking for during the initial test window:

- Cost per booked call within your target range

- Hook rate above 20% on video ads

- Unique outbound CTR within benchmark (1 to 2%+ for video, 2 to 4%+ for static)

- Frequency under 1.5

- ROAS positive (or at minimum, on track to be positive given your sales cycle length)

After 5 to 7 days, you decide: scale, kill, or hold.

Scale: ads that are within or below your target cost per result, with healthy CTR and hook rates. Increase budget gradually, no more than 20 to 30% per increase, and only after another few days of stable performance at the new spend level.

Kill: ads that are dramatically over your target cost per result, with CTR or hook rates well below benchmark. Don't try to fix dying ads. Replace them with the next batch.

Hold: ads that are borderline. Wait another few days and reassess. Don't make impulsive decisions on partial data.

Most ads will fall into the kill bucket. That's normal. The hit rate on creative testing is around 10%, meaning 1 in 10 ads will be a real winner. The other 9 are necessary failures that produced data and ruled out angles. Keep producing batches, keep testing, keep killing the losers, and the winners will emerge.
