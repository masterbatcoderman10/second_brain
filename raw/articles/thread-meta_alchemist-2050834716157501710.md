60 GitHub accounts every AI builder should follow to be great at AI

A curated walkthrough of the people behind the tools you already use, plus a playbook for translating their research into products you can ship.

Most builders who say they're "in AI" rarely open the GitHub of the people actually building the field. They follow launch threads, demo clips, and influencer commentary. They miss the source.

This article is a curated walk-through of 60 of those accounts. Real people, not company logos, mapped across the seven layers of the AI stack you touch every day.

For each account you'll get a short, honest read: what they built, why the work matters, and the part most "follow these accounts" lists skip, what you as a builder can actually take from their work and turn into your own.

A repo to run, a paper to read, a gap in the ecosystem to fill, a tiny product nobody else is building yet.

Save this. Open two profiles per day for the next 30 days. Research what they have built deeply. By the end of the month, you'll have a sharper map of the AI world, a working short list of repos worth cloning, patterns of the AI world, and blue oceans to claim. 

That's the difference between watching the AI moment from the outside and actually being part of it.

## How to read this list to actually get value out of it

Following 60 accounts and feeling smarter is a trap. The point of this list is not to crowd your feed. The point is to use these profiles as doorways into repos, patterns, and ideas you can build on. Here's how to do that without wasting your time:

1. Don't just star the repo, read the design choices

The interesting part of any repo on this list is rarely the code itself. It's the architecture decisions in the README, the issues thread where the maintainer explained why they said no to a PR, the design doc nobody else read. Skim the code. Read the why. That's where the lesson is.

2. Look for the unfair advantage they had

Every builder on this list shipped because of one specific edge: deep domain knowledge, perfect timing, focused execution, or a willingness to do unfun infra work everyone else avoided. Spot the edge. Then ask yourself which kind of edge you have, and what you should be building because of it.

3. Run their code, break it, then ship something using it

Pick one repo per week from this list. Clone it. Run it locally. Break it on purpose. Ship something tiny on top of it by Friday. Reading repos is not the same as understanding them, and understanding them is not the same as having shipped with them.

4. Treat their work as your starter kit, not their finished product

Most of the products on this list are missing a layer the average vibe coder can build in a weekend. A nicer UI on top of llama.cpp. A vertical-specific wrapper around aider. A productized agent built on the BabyAGI loop. The "boring layer on top of brilliant code" is one of the highest-leverage moves in the AI economy right now.

5. Translate research to revenue

Every paper implementation on this list represents a real productization opportunity. FlashAttention spawned a whole inference economy. Mamba is spawning the next one. The pattern is consistent: when a paper drops a new technique, the first three people to ship a clean product around it usually own the category.

6. Follow them, then follow who they follow

The AI ecosystem is smaller than it looks. The people on this list mostly know each other, build on each other's work, and signal-boost each other's research. Follow these 60, then follow 5 to 10 of who each of them follows. Your feed becomes a frontier scanner instead of a hype filter.

Group 1: Foundational and classic

## 1. [karpathy](https://github.com/karpathy), Andrej Karpathy

He's the closest thing the AI world has to a teacher you can actually learn from. Every project he drops, from nanoGPT to llm.c to micrograd, walks you through how transformers really work, line by readable line. No PhD required.

If you want to go from "I use AI tools" to "I understand what's happening inside the model", follow him. You'll be 10x more dangerous as a builder once you know the machinery underneath.

What you can build from this: rebuild nanoGPT yourself in a weekend, then write a tutorial of your own from the experience. Teaching what he teaches in your own voice is one of the highest-conversion content angles in AI right now.

## 2. [ggerganov](https://github.com/ggerganov), Georgi Gerganov

One developer made it possible to run massive LLMs on a laptop. That's llama.cpp, and Georgi built it almost solo while everyone else was waiting for cloud GPUs.

Follow him if you care about local AI, on-device inference, or just want proof of what one focused builder can ship. His code sits under Ollama, llamafile, and half the local AI tools you already use.

What you can build from this: a vertical product on top of llama.cpp for a specific user (lawyers, doctors, accountants) who can't send data to the cloud. The runtime is solved, the verticalization is wide open.

## 3. [geohot](https://github.com/geohot), George Hotz

Jailbroke the iPhone at 17, now building tinygrad to compete with PyTorch. He ships in public, streams the work live, and doesn't care who he upsets along the way.

Follow him for raw building energy and contrarian takes on the AI infra stack. You won't always agree with him, but you'll always learn something.

What you can build from this: a clean tutorial on tinygrad for a specific use case. The framework is great, the educational layer is thin, and that gap is yours to take.

## 4. [soumith](https://github.com/soumith), Soumith Chintala

Co-creator of PyTorch, the framework almost every serious AI lab on earth runs on. Quiet, technical, and respected by everyone who matters in the field.

Follow him to see where deep learning infrastructure is heading, from someone actually shaping it. His takes on hardware, training, and open ecosystems usually land before the trend does.

What you can build from this: watch what he's signal-boosting. The frameworks and hardware he points at six months early are the ones you should be skill-stacking on now.

## 5. [fchollet](https://github.com/fchollet), François Chollet

Built Keras, which made deep learning accessible to millions. Now he runs the ARC challenge, the cleanest benchmark for whether a model is actually reasoning or just memorizing.

Follow him for sharp, principled thinking about what AI is and isn't. He'll keep your head clear when the hype cycle gets loud.

What you can build from this: tooling around ARC. Visualizers, dataset explorers, leaderboards, agents that attempt the puzzles. The benchmark is the new frontier and the toolchain around it is barely built.

## 6. [ylecun](https://github.com/ylecun), Yann LeCun

Turing Award winner, Meta's Chief AI Scientist, and the guy who keeps loudly disagreeing with the "LLMs are everything" crowd. Convolutional networks exist because of him.

Follow him for a counterweight to the daily AI hype cycle. He's been right about a lot of things long before they were consensus.

What you can build from this: read what he's skeptical about. Some of the biggest opportunities in AI right now sit exactly in the lanes the consensus is overhyping and a few sober voices are quietly warning about.

## 7. [lucidrains](https://github.com/lucidrains), Phil Wang

Reimplements almost every important AI paper in clean PyTorch, usually within days of it dropping. One person, hundreds of repos, real quality across all of them.

Follow him if you'd rather understand papers through code than math. Fastest way to grasp new architectures without doing a PhD.

What you can build from this: turn his bare-bones implementations into productized notebooks, courses, or developer kits. He ships the science, the educational and product layer is wide open.

## 8. [rwightman](https://github.com/rwightman), Ross Wightman

Built timm, the library every computer vision team quietly depends on. It powers a huge slice of production vision work today.

Follow him if vision models are anywhere near your stack. He shares benchmarks, training tricks, and model comparisons you won't find elsewhere.

What you can build from this: a vertical computer vision product (inventory counting, document parsing, defect detection) using timm as the backbone. The model zoo is solved, the deployment-ready vertical apps are not.

## 9. [srush](https://github.com/srush), Sasha Rush

Cornell professor who turns hard ML topics into things a normal builder can use. The Annotated Transformer and MiniTorch are both his.

Follow him if you learn better through worked examples than dense papers. He's the bridge between research and practitioners who actually ship.

What you can build from this: follow his pattern. Pick one ML topic, build the cleanest "annotated" version of it on the public web, and you'll have a portfolio piece that opens doors for years.

## 10. [rasbt](https://github.com/rasbt), Sebastian Raschka

Author of "Build a Large Language Model from Scratch", one of the cleanest learning paths in the field. Hands-on, clear, zero fluff.

Follow him if you want to actually understand LLMs instead of just calling APIs. Every post is a small free lesson in modern ML.

What you can build from this: his book is a content treasure chest. Each chapter is a YouTube video, a thread, a workshop, or a paid cohort waiting to be made for an audience that learns better with a different teacher.

Group 2: Model training and efficiency

Co-author of FlashAttention and Mamba, two of the most important papers of the last few years. Any fast inference setup you're using probably has his code somewhere in it.

Follow him if you care about how the next generation of models will run. He's at the frontier of making them faster and cheaper.

What you can build from this: any product whose unit economics depend on cheaper inference (voice agents, video pipelines, real-time personalization). The papers he ships compound directly into your margins.

## 12. [albertfgu](https://github.com/albertfgu), Albert Gu

The other half of Mamba, and the leading researcher on state space models, the architecture genuinely challenging transformers. His work could shape what comes after attention.

Follow him if you want to be early on the next architecture wave. He's been building toward it for years.

## 13. [TimDettmers](https://github.com/TimDettmers), Tim Dettmers

## 14. [danielhanchen](https://github.com/danielhanchen), Daniel Han

Follow him if you finetune anything at all. Every thread he posts is a free optimization for your training loop.

What you can build from this: a finetuning-as-a-service business for a vertical (legal AI, medical AI, sales AI) sitting on top of Unsloth. The cost reduction is the moat.

## 15. [winglian](https://github.com/winglian), Wing Lian

Maintainer of Axolotl, the default open source framework for finetuning LLMs. If a Hugging Face model was finetuned in the last two years, Axolotl was probably involved.

Follow him for no-nonsense takes on training pipelines. He's solving the boring infra problems that make finetuning actually work in practice.

What you can build from this: turnkey Axolotl recipes for specific use cases (function-calling, structured output, domain-specific assistants). Recipe libraries are content gold and lead magnets at the same time.

## 16. [teknium1](https://github.com/teknium1), Teknium

Core researcher at Nous Research, the most respected open weights crew in the game. Hermes, OpenHermes, and a long list of finetunes that punch way above their weight.

Follow him if you care about open models that aren't gatekept by big labs. He sits at the center of the open AI ecosystem.

What you can build from this: products that depend on open weights for cost or sovereignty reasons. His finetunes are production-ready substitutes for a lot of GPT-4-class workloads.

## 17. [mlabonne](https://github.com/mlabonne), Maxime Labonne

Built one of the best free LLM courses online and pioneered model merging as a serious technique. His repos are some of the cleanest learning material in the space.

Follow him if you want a real path from "I read about LLMs" to "I'm shipping finetuned models". He shows every step of the work.

## 18. [ehartford](https://github.com/ehartford), Eric Hartford

The mind behind Dolphin, the Cognitive Computations crew, and a long line of uncensored finetunes. He also writes some of the clearest essays on alignment, openness, and what these models really are.

> The bigger pattern across these 60

Almost everyone on this list shipped something tiny first, then iterated for years. nanoGPT was one Python file. llama.cpp started as a weekend port. AutoGPT was a viral hack week project. 

They didn't predict the wave, they just kept building useful, focused things and the wave came to them. Take that personally.
