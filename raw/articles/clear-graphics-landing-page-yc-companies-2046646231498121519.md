# the landing page structure that 5 of the top YC funded companies use (full breakdown)

the landing page structure that 5 of the most successful yc companies use from the last few batches.

the structure is almost IDENTICAL across all of them.

here's the exact pattern:

you've probably heard of the companies: 
- resend (developer email infrastructure)
- chatbase (ai chatbot builder)
- artisan (ai sdr)
- deel (global payroll)
- vanta (security compliance)

they have completely different products and icp but the page structure is nearly identical

pattern 1: one-line hero with product screenshot

every single one of these companies has a hero section with a headline under 10 words and a real screenshot of the product in action

it NEEDS to be the actual ui

chatbase shows their chatbot builder interface.

resend shows their email dashboard

artisan shows their ai agent in action

vanta shows their compliance dashboard

why this works:

it immediately answers "what does this product look like" which is the first question every visitor has.

screenshots also build trust because they prove the product exists and is actually polished

pattern 2: social proof within the first scroll

ALL five companies show customer logos or a usage metric before you scroll past the first viewport

resend: "trusted by the best teams." plus developer tool logos

chatbase: trusted by 10k businesses worldwide

artisan: before/after and number of people using it

deel: "40,000+ companies"

vanta: recognizable security-conscious brand logos

this social proof appears within 500 pixels of the page top

its IMMEDIATELY visible

pattern 3: one clear cta repeated

every page has ONE primary action.

"get started" or "start free trial" or "book a demo."

this same cta appears in the hero, after the first feature section, and at the bottom of the page

none of them have competing actions in the hero.

no "watch a demo" alongside "get started" bullshit

they have one action that is repeated consistently throughout the page so the visitor never has to scroll back up to convert

pattern 4: founder photos somewhere on the page

this one surprised me because 4 out of 5 had founder photos or team photos visible on the landing page.

they were usually in an "about" or "backed by" section

why this matters for startups specifically:

investors and early adopters NEED to know who's behind the product.

a face makes the company feel real and approachable

faceless startups feel risky asf (and lifeless)

at the early stage this is a huge trust signal.

and at later stages (like deel) it shifts to investor logos and press mentions

but early on, showing your face is one of the most (maybe the most) underrated conversion tactics

pattern 5: pricing visible and simple

4 out of 5 had pricing visible on the landing page or one click away and even the ones with enterprise pricing showed a free tier or starting price

hidden pricing is a conversion KILLER for developer tools and smb products

these companies know their buyers want to self-qualify immediately.

putting up a "contact sales" wall at the early stage is a mistake unless your minimum deal size is like $50k+

pattern 6: feature sections limited to 3-6

none of these pages listed more than 4 core features on the main page. each feature had its own section with a visual, a headline, and 1-2 sentences of explanation

resend shows 6 features. chatbase shows 5-6. artisan shows 3 with a before/after comparison

they all understand that fewer features presented well DESTROYS a long feature list that nobody reads

pattern 7: the page ends with action not information

every page ends with a final cta block.

has to be a clear call to action with a benefit driven headline and an actual button or link lol

"start building in minutes" or "get compliant faster" and then the button

should be the last thing the visitor sees before they either convert or leave

the visitors who scroll to the bottom of your page are your warmest traffic and the final cta is your last chance to convert them so make it count

the framework summarized:

hero (headline + screenshot + cta) → social proof bar → problem statement → 3-4 feature sections → testimonial → how it works → pricing → founder/team → final cta

this structure fucking prints because it follows how the brain processes a purchase decision:

what is this → who uses it → what problem does it solve → how does it work → what does it cost → who built it → should i try it
