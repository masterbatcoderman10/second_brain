## The Offer Architecture

The first thing you notice when you study Viral Coach's setup is how broad their target market is.

They don't run ads to a tightly defined avatar like "law firm partners doing $2M to $5M per year in revenue." They go after every B2B business owner doing meaningful revenue who isn't capitalising on social media. Law firms, accounting firms, finance firms, plumbing companies, contractors, consultancies. The breadth is intentional, and it's one of the structural decisions that lets them scale spend without hitting the ceiling that kills most agency ad accounts.

Here's the principle that explains why this works.

Meta is a shotgun, not a sniper rifle.

If you're trying to reach a tightly specified avatar (say, e-commerce brand owners doing exactly $500k to $2M per month in a specific category), your CPMs and your cost per booked call are going to climb significantly. The reason is simple: there's a small population of those exact people on Meta, and there are usually multiple competitors in your space bidding for the same audience. When multiple advertisers compete for a small audience, ad costs rise and ad fatigue accelerates because the same audience keeps seeing your ads on repeat.

Most agency owners don't realise how small their actual addressable audience is on Meta until they try to scale spend. They imagine they're targeting "all e-commerce brand owners," but their real audience is something like "US-based e-commerce brand owners doing $300k to $2M per month in a specific niche, who are decision-makers and active on Meta." That's not a TAM of millions. That might be 30,000 to 50,000 people, and once your campaign has shown ads to all of them 5 to 10 times, performance starts to fall off a cliff.

Viral Coach sidesteps this entirely. By targeting B2B business owners broadly, they have a TAM of literally millions of people, which means they can scale ad spend to levels most agencies can't reach without burning their audience out.

The trade-off is that their offer has to be flexible enough to appeal to that broad market. Which brings us to the second structural choice.

One offer. High ticket. Take it or leave it.

Most agencies have 4 or 5 different service tiers, with multiple price points and complicated sales processes. Viral Coach has 1 offer. It's tens of thousands of dollars. Either you fit it or you don't.

This works because of how their TAM is structured. When you're going after the top 1% of B2B business owners with millions of dollars to spend, you don't need a low-ticket entry offer. You need 1 high-ticket offer that justifies the marketing spend required to reach those people in the first place.

There's a useful trade-off principle worth noticing here. You can either have:

- A small TAM with multiple offers at different price points (maximising revenue per buyer in a constrained audience)

- A massive TAM with 1 offer (maximising the number of buyers from a less constrained audience)

Both work. Viral Coach is firmly in the second camp, and it's one of the reasons they can sustain hundreds of new clients a month.

## Why They Pivoted Out of Coaching

There's an interesting backstory here that's worth flagging because it's directly relevant to anyone considering whether their current niche is right for paid ads at scale.

Viral Coach originally started in the coaching niche. They were targeting online coaches and helping them grow their content. The problem they ran into is that there simply weren't enough coaches with the budget required for their high-ticket offer. The market wasn't big enough to support the volume of clients they needed to scale meaningfully.

So they pivoted. They went broader (any B2B business doing decent revenue) and they went after a less educated audience.

This second piece is critical and worth dwelling on. Coaches are sophisticated when it comes to social media. They know what content is, they understand virality, they have opinions about hooks and editing. They're a tough sell because they think they could do it themselves and they have strong opinions about what "good" looks like.

A 7-figure law firm partner has none of that context. They know they should be on social media, they understand it's important, but they have no idea how to actually produce content that gets views and converts. They're a much easier sell because the gap between what they need and what they currently have is massive and obvious.

This is a generalisable principle for any agency considering their target market. If you're stuck on a sophisticated, marketing-aware audience, going broader and going after a less educated segment of the same problem space often produces dramatically better unit economics and dramatically easier sales calls.

## The Creative Strategy

Once you spend time inside Viral Coach's ad library, the most striking thing is the sheer volume of creative variation. Talking head ads. Podcast-format ads. UGC ads. B-roll ads. Static images. Founder ads featuring Daniel directly. Founder ads featuring other team members.

The variation is intimidating at first glance. It looks like they're doing 6 or 7 different things at once and managing some kind of impossibly complex creative pipeline.

But once you cut through the format variety, what you actually see is the same core messaging repeated across different formats. The hook that works in a talking head ad is the same hook that anchors a static ad. The pain point that drives one piece of UGC content is the same pain point articulated through a podcast clip. The format varies. The angle stays consistent.

This is the principle that I think most agencies miss when they study what bigger ad accounts are doing.

Static ads are creative in their rawest form.

When I'm reverse-engineering a competitor's ad account, I always start with their static ads. The reason is that statics force the advertiser to communicate the entire angle in a single image with maybe 1 or 2 lines of text. There's nowhere to hide. If the static is "posting daily but still not getting clients" with a frustrated-looking person, that's a clear pain point. If the static is "the million view guarantee" with a bold callout, that's a clear desire/benefit hook.

Once you've identified the core angles powering an account through their statics, the video ads become much easier to understand. They're the same angles, expanded into 60 to 90 second formats with more proof, more authority, and more direct CTAs.

The "million view guarantee" is Viral Coach's strongest hook by some margin. You see it in their statics, you see it in their video ads, and (as we'll get to in a moment) you see it as the headline on their landing page. The messaging stays constant because the messaging is what's been validated. The format just changes based on which surface you're advertising on.

The 30/30/30/10 Creative Mix

For a mature ad account spending over $1,000 per day, here's the creative split I'd recommend, and it's what Viral Coach appears to be running:

30% direct offer ads. Founder talking directly to camera, presenting the offer clearly. "We'll get you a million views or you don't pay. Here's how."

30% case study and testimonial ads. Before-and-after results, screenshots of client wins, founder breaking down specific case studies with numbers.

30% educational top-of-funnel ads. UGC-style or podcast-format content that teaches something useful while soft-selling the offer.

10% wildcard. Random, off-the-cuff, weird creative that breaks the usual patterns. Sometimes these become your best-performing ads precisely because they don't look like ads.

If you're a smaller account spending under $500 per day, you don't need this much variety. Start with direct offer ads only and stay there until you have at least 1 winner spending profitably. The reason direct offer ads should dominate early is simple. They convert the bottom 2 to 3% of your market that's actively looking for the solution you sell. That audience exists in any market and it's the easiest to convert.

But once you scale spend past $1,000 per day, you start running out of those bottom-of-funnel buyers. Your ads have shown to all of them. To keep scaling, you have to expand into colder audiences who aren't yet problem-aware, and that requires educational and case study content rather than direct pitches.

This is exactly the pattern Viral Coach is following. Their direct offer ads anchor the bottom of the funnel. Their UGC and case study content goes after the colder audiences they need to reach to keep scaling spend without the cost per call climbing through the roof.

## The Primary Text Insight That Saves Hours of Work

Here's something specific I noticed across Viral Coach's ad library that's worth flagging because it contradicts what most agencies obsess over.

They reuse the same primary text ad copy across dozens of different ads.

Once they found primary text that works, they basically stopped iterating on it. Same opening lines. Same body. Same CTA. Repeated across talking head ads, static ads, podcast ads, UGC ads. The ad creative changes, but the primary text stays constant.

This pattern shows up across pretty much every high-spending agency ad account I've audited (and I have visibility into 30 to 40 different agency accounts across various niches through my consulting work at The Advertising Co).

The primary text is not what moves the needle.

99% of your creative testing energy should go into the ad creative itself, the video, the static, the visual hook. The primary text is supporting infrastructure. Once you've found something that works, leave it alone and pour the iteration time into testing new creative angles.

That said, the primary text Viral Coach uses is genuinely good copy. It taps into a latent desire most B2B business owners have but rarely articulate explicitly: the awareness that they're not capitalising on social media in a way that bigger players in their space are. They reference how Hormozi and Grant Cardone spend hundreds of thousands of dollars per month on content teams. They acknowledge that their target buyer (a law firm partner, a tax advisor, a finance firm owner) hasn't allocated resources to a content team because they're specialists in their actual profession, not in marketing. The copy meets the prospect exactly where they are mentally.

The other thing the copy does well is decreasing perceived risk. For Viral Coach's target buyer, the money for the offer isn't the issue (they're 7 to 8 figure businesses). What holds them back is the perception that creating content well requires expertise they don't have. So Viral Coach's messaging consistently addresses that specifically. They're not asking the buyer to figure out content. They're saying "we'll handle the entire production process and even place a content specialist into your business."

This is almost certainly pulled directly from sales call objections. If you're not already doing this in your own ad copy work, here's the process I recommend:

1. Record every sales call (using Fathom, Fireflies, or a similar tool)

2. Once a week, dump the transcripts into Claude or ChatGPT

3. Ask the model to extract the top reasons prospects cited for getting on the call, the top reasons they cited for buying, and the top objections they raised, ranked by frequency

4. Use that output as ammunition for ad copy, hooks, and overall messaging

This single workflow, repeated weekly, produces better ad copy than hours of brainstorming because it's grounded in what your actual market is saying, not what you assume they care about.

## The Funnel Architecture: VSL Funnel and Short-Form Funnel

Viral Coach runs 2 main funnel types, and the structural choice between them is worth understanding because it directly affects your cost per call and your lead quality.

The VSL Funnel

Headline above the fold. Very simple. "A million views, or you don't pay." That's it. No 2-line headlines, no subheading underneath the headline, no preamble. The winning hook from their best-performing ad is now the headline on their landing page, and it's also the central premise of the VSL itself.

This is one of the most important principles I've internalised from studying high-performing ad accounts.

Marketing assets are interchangeable. The hook that works in your ad is the headline on your landing page. The headline on your landing page is the opening of your VSL. The opening of your VSL is the framing of your sales call. Once you've validated a piece of messaging, you push it through every surface in your funnel because it works.

Most agencies treat their ad creative, their landing page, and their VSL as 3 separate pieces of work that need different copy. They don't. They're 3 different formats of the same core message, and they should all reinforce each other.

The VSL itself is under 5 minutes long. Short, sweet, calculated. Daniel knows his target buyer (decision-makers in busy 7-figure businesses) doesn't have 30 minutes to watch a long-form sales letter. So the VSL is built to sell the call in as little time as possible.

The VSL also handles a specific objection that Viral Coach almost certainly hears constantly on sales calls. The objection is: "A million views sounds great, but how does that actually translate into revenue for my business?" That's a real concern any sophisticated buyer would raise, and rather than waiting for it to come up on the call, Daniel addresses it directly inside the VSL. The framing is essentially: "We don't just specialise in views, we specialise in converting views into revenue." Objection handled before the prospect ever speaks to a human.

Below the VSL, the page is intentionally minimal:

- A row of client logos for social proof

- A clearly visible CTA button

- An application form with qualifying questions

- A long scroll of results/testimonials

That's it. No FAQ section. No "our process" breakdown. No problem/solution narrative. No team page.

This is a deliberate design choice that most agencies get wrong. The longer and more complicated your funnel, the more you distract people from the 1 thing the page actually needs to do, which is get them to watch the VSL and book a call. Every additional section is another reason for someone to scroll past the VSL or click away before completing the form.

Above the fold on mobile, the VSL, the button, and the application form are all visible without scrolling. The prospect can engage with the offer immediately without doing any work.

The Short-Form Funnel

Viral Coach also runs a short-form funnel variation, typically used when they're running ads with a different brand face (someone other than Daniel). The reason for this is consistency. If the ad has a face that's not Daniel and the VSL has Daniel in it, there's a disconnect that hurts conversion. The short-form funnel solves this by removing the VSL entirely.

Structure of the short-form funnel:

- Headline (the proven hook again)

- Subheading

- Button

- Application form

- Client logos and testimonials below

No VSL. No long-form copy. Just the proven messaging and the path to book a call.

I personally run short-form funnels regularly and they consistently produce cheaper costs per booked call than VSL funnels. VSLs add friction. Even a 5-minute video is 5 minutes that a busy decision-maker has to carve out of their day. Many of them mentally bookmark the VSL as "I'll watch this later" and then never come back.

The trade-off is that prospects who book through a short-form funnel are typically less warmed up than those who watched a full VSL. They arrive on the call needing more education, which puts more pressure on the sales process and the pre-call nurture system to do the warming work that the VSL would have done.

For Viral Coach, this trade-off is fine because they have a very strong pre-call nurture system (which we'll get to next). For an agency without that infrastructure built out, a VSL funnel is usually the safer starting point because it does more selling on autopilot.

## The Application Form: 3-Tier Lead Routing

Viral Coach's application form asks a specific set of qualifying questions:

- First name and last name

- Email and phone number

- Monthly revenue

- Marketing spend

- Average customer lifetime value

- When are you able to start

These questions aren't just for filtering. They're for scoring and routing.

Every lead coming through the funnel gets categorised into 1 of 3 tiers based on their answers:

- Tier 1 are the highest-quality leads (largest businesses, highest budgets, ready to start immediately). These go directly to the most senior closers.

- Tier 2 are mid-quality leads. They go to junior closers, or to senior setters who can pre-qualify them further before passing them up.

- Tier 3 are lower-quality leads. They don't get sales calls. They go to setter follow-up sequences, long-term email nurture, or the daily newsletter.

This tiering system is one of the highest-leverage operational decisions a high-volume agency can make. It maximises revenue per closer hour. Your best closers spend their time on your best leads. Your less experienced team members handle the leads that need more qualification work.

If you're spending $5,000+ per month on ads and not tiering your leads, you're almost certainly leaving meaningful revenue on the table by sending mediocre leads to your best closers and burning their calendar capacity.

## The Thank You Page

After someone completes the application form, they land on a thank you page that walks them through exactly what to expect next.

The structure:

- A short video (60 to 90 seconds) walking through next steps

- Confirmation that the team will reach out

- An instruction to accept the calendar invite

- An instruction to come prepared to talk strategy

This is where the schedule pixel fires (assuming Viral Coach is optimising their campaigns toward booked calls, which they almost certainly are).

The thank you page is also the highest-intent moment in the entire funnel. The prospect just committed to a call. Their motivation is at peak right now. This is when you reinforce the decision and start the trust-building process that needs to carry them through the next 24 to 48 hours until the call happens.

## The Pre-Call Nurture System

Viral Coach's pre-call sequence has 3 main components:

1. Email Nurture Sequence

Triggered the moment the calendar event is booked. A series of emails providing value, addressing common objections, and reinforcing the offer before the call.

At Paid House, we sent 9 emails between booking and the call, which is on the higher end. Viral Coach appears to send fewer (probably 5 to 7), but the principle is the same: get prospects more educated before they show up so the call itself becomes a fit-confirmation conversation rather than an information delivery session.

Open rates on these emails should sit between 50 and 70% if your sequence is well-built. Anything below that suggests the subject lines need work or the sequence is too long.

2. Manual Outreach Via Messaging

Viral Coach reaches out to prospects via WhatsApp. We use iMessage at Paid House and The Advertising Co. The platform matters less than the principle: the closer (or a setter assigned to the closer) reaches out personally before the call to introduce themselves, build rapport, and ask for any prep information that would make the call more productive.

Tools that make this manageable at scale include Link, Sendle, and similar multi-line messaging platforms that let your team manage conversations from a unified inbox while preserving the personal feel of a direct text.

3. Dedicated SDRs / Setters

This is where Viral Coach's system shows its operational maturity. They have setters dedicated to 3 specific functions:

Function 1: Calling and texting applicants who fill out the application but don't book the call. The SOP is to dial within 5 minutes of the partial submission. If no answer, dial again the same day. If no answer, try again 24 hours later. After that, the lead goes into long-term email nurture.

Function 2: Calling and texting people who cancel or no-show. The setter dials them daily for up to 5 days to attempt to reschedule. After 5 days with no response, the lead goes into the long-term nurture system.

Function 3: Calling people who successfully book to confirm the meeting and ask discovery questions. This serves multiple purposes. It gets confirmation that the prospect will actually show up. It surfaces additional context the closer can use during the call. And for borderline-quality leads, it lets the setter make a judgment call on whether the lead is worth the closer's time or whether the call should be cancelled.

This 3-function setter structure is one of the most underrated operational systems in high-volume agency sales. Most agencies that spend significantly on ads don't have this infrastructure built out, which means they're losing 20 to 30% of their potential bookings to no-shows, partial submits, and unconfirmed calls that simply never happen.

## The Takeaways for Your Agency

If you take nothing else from this breakdown, take these:

Broad TAM with 1 high-ticket offer scales further than narrow TAM with multiple offers. Most agencies are constraining their TAM unnecessarily by being too specific about who they target.

Format variation matters less than messaging consistency. The same hooks should appear across your ads, your landing page, your VSL, and your sales process. Once a piece of messaging is validated, push it through every surface in your funnel.

Start with direct offer ads. Add variety only as you scale. Below $1,000/day in spend, direct offer ads are where the easiest conversions live. Educational and case study content matters more as you push past $1,000/day and start running out of bottom-of-funnel buyers.

Reuse primary text. Iterate on creative. The primary text is supporting infrastructure. The visual creative is what moves the needle. Don't waste hours rewriting copy when the conversion lever is the video itself.

Keep your funnel ruthlessly short. VSL, button, application, social proof. That's the structure. Every additional section is a reason for prospects to bounce.

Tier your leads. Route them to the right people. Your best closers should spend their time on your best leads. Setters should handle mid and low-tier leads.

Build a 3-function setter system. Calling partial submits, recovering no-shows, and confirming booked calls. This single piece of infrastructure can recover 20 to 30% of bookings most agencies lose silently.
